üîÑ The Lifecycle of Computing Technology

The environmental impact spans the entire lifecycle ‚Äî from extracting raw materials right through to disposal. The average smartphone lifespan is less than 2 years, meaning rapid cycles of new manufacturing and e-waste.

‚õèÔ∏è Stage 1 & 2: Raw Materials & Manufacturing

Computing devices require many different raw materials: sand (glass for screens), oil (plastics for casings), and various metals including gold, copper, silver and palladium. Some metals used (uranium, thorium) are radioactive and can contaminate air, soil and groundwater. Rare-earth metals (China produces 90%+) have unique magnetic and electrochemical properties that improve computing performance.

üç™ Cookies vs Spyware
üç™ Cookies
Small data files stored in your browser. Record: websites visited, products viewed or bought, preferences. Enable personalised services. EU law gives right to opt out.
üïµÔ∏è Spyware

üî≠ Big Data

Individual pieces of data seem harmless (phone number, music download, hobbies). But when combined, they produce a very accurate, detailed profile revealing far more than you intended to disclose. This could lead to identity theft or social engineering.

27üíº Professionalism
‚ñº
KEY TERMS Professionalism ‚Äì the skill and competence expected of a person in a professional setting.
Ethics ‚Äì a set of moral principles that govern behaviour (what is right vs wrong).
Start-up ‚Äì a company in the first stage of developing a product or service for which there is demand.

Computer scientists work across many settings ‚Äî multinational companies (Microsoft, Apple), start-ups, hospitals, universities, and as self-employed individuals. Their work can literally put lives at risk (e.g. embedded systems in cars, medical devices), making ethics especially critical.

BCS CODE OF CONDUCT The BCS (British Computer Society) is the Chartered Institute for IT. Members pay annual fees to access specialist conferences, training and publications. The BCS Code of Conduct specifies members must:
  • Always respect the wellbeing, privacy and security of the environment and people they work with
  • Never stop learning ‚Äî continuously gain skills and professional knowledge
  • Behave ethically and with integrity at all times
  • Abide by all relevant laws and regulations
  • Act in the public interest ‚Äî not just their employer's
üöó Ethical Dilemmas ‚Äî Car Brakes Embedded System

A computer scientist developing an embedded system to control car brakes may face situations where legal and ethical considerations clash:

Scenario 1: Bug Found
She spots a bug but fixing it will delay the project deadline. She MUST report it ‚Äî shipping unsafe brakes could cause deaths. Public safety must come before deadlines.
Scenario 2: Security Flaw
She discovers someone could remotely hack the brakes. Even if the probability is low, she MUST disclose this risk ‚Äî a professional cannot hide a safety vulnerability from the public.
Scenario 3: Bribed by Competitor
A competitor offers to pay for the code. She MUST refuse ‚Äî sharing proprietary code breaches her employment contract, confidentiality agreements, and is likely illegal under IP law.
Scenario 4: False Claims
Her employer makes untrue claims about the system's safety capabilities. She must challenge this ‚Äî allowing false claims would be unethical and potentially illegal under consumer protection law.
üí° An action can be legal but not ethical. E.g. it's legal to leave an old computer in the attic. But if someone in a developing country would benefit from it, the ethical thing is to pass it on. Computer scientists must make judgements that go beyond just following the law.

üìÑ Software Licensing

Every piece of software ‚Äî even free software ‚Äî has a licence. Even though you purchase software, the licence states you don't actually own it ‚Äî you purchase the right to use it under the licence terms. The manufacturer retains ownership.

A software licence specifies: how many copies you can use ¬∑ whether installable on multiple computers ¬∑ which type of organisation can use it (some are for charities, students or home users only) ¬∑ how long it can be used (perpetual licences last forever; subscription licences expire unless renewed).

üîê Computer Misuse Acts & Cybersecurity

Many countries have Computer Misuse Acts (CMAs) making it illegal to access computer systems without permission. These include countries in Europe, the GCC, Australia and Singapore. ‚öõÔ∏è 4. Quantum Computing
KEY TERMS Quantum mechanics ‚Äì the branch of physics describing behaviour of subatomic particles where normal physics laws don't apply.
Superposition ‚Äì a quantum system can be in multiple states at the same time, until it is measured.
Qubit ‚Äì a quantum bit; can be 0, 1, or both simultaneously (unlike a classical bit which is either 0 or 1).
Entanglement ‚Äì pairs of qubits share a quantum state; changing one immediately changes the other, even over huge distances.

Quantum computers are not just faster classical computers ‚Äî they are based on a completely different reality. They use qubits which can be 0, 1, or both at the same time (superposition). IBM and Google use superconducting circuits cooled to temperatures colder than deep space to generate qubits.

üîç Click to zoom ¬∑ ‚¨á Download available in lightbox
üê± Schr√∂dinger's Cat: A famous thought experiment illustrating superposition. A cat placed in a sealed box with cyanide is both dead and alive simultaneously until the box is opened (observed). Similarly, a qubit is both 0 and 1 until measured ‚Äî then it collapses to one value.

Entanglement: Pairs of qubits share a quantum state ‚Äî changing one immediately changes the other, over any distance. Einstein called this "spooky action at a distance." In a quantum computer, entangled qubits produce an exponential increase in computing power.

Challenge: Qubits are fragile ‚Äî even tiny vibrations or temperature changes cause "noise", shifting qubits out of superposition and causing errors. This is why qubits must be cooled to temperatures colder than deep space.
